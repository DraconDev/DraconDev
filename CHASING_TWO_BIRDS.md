# Chasing Two Birds: Job Signal vs Business Signal

**Date:** 2026-06-12  
**Purpose:** Explain why job-seeking and business-building are nearly opposite public signals, and how DraconDev should frame that tension honestly.

## Core angle

The “two birds” are:

1. **Getting a good job.**
2. **Building a dev business / productized assets.**

They can both be true in someone’s life, but they are not the same public signal.

A job-seeking signal says:

> “Please evaluate me as a candidate. I am available, reliable, and ready to join your team.”

A business-building signal says:

> “I build and own products. The work is the proof. Use it, buy it, support it, or partner with me.”

Those signals are almost opposite.

If DraconDev tries to serve both equally, the profile becomes confusing. Employers may wonder whether this is a job candidate, a freelancer, a founder, a course seller, a maintainer, or a random repo collector. Product buyers may wonder whether they are looking at a resume instead of a product.

The chosen angle remains:

> **DraconDev is primarily product/business-first. Employment is welcome only when it fits the product-maker trajectory.**

## The uncomfortable truth

Trying to build a business can make DraconDev look less employable to some employers.

This is not only about lacking a normal job history. It is about the signal:

- “This person may leave if the product gains traction.”
- “This person may be too independent.”
- “This person may not want a manager.”
- “This person may care more about their own roadmap than company priorities.”
- “This person may be a founder-type, not an employee-type.”
- “This person may not fit a conventional team structure.”

Some employers will dislike that. That is not a bug in every case.

It is a filter.

## When this is a feature

Business-first positioning is a feature when DraconDev wants to attract:

- product companies,
- technical founders,
- small teams that value ownership,
- infrastructure teams that respect shipped systems,
- buyers/users of tools,
- sponsors/supporters,
- collaborators who want a builder, not a resume.

For those audiences, the business signal is stronger than the job signal.

It says:

> “This person does not just talk about building. They ship, package, maintain, and try to turn work into a product.”

That is valuable.

## When this is a bug

Business-first positioning is a bug when DraconDev is trying to appeal to:

- conventional corporate hiring filters,
- recruiters scanning for stable job history,
- managers who want a predictable employee narrative,
- teams that distrust founder-like candidates,
- roles where product ownership would be seen as a flight risk.

If the immediate goal is a conventional job, the public profile should not be optimized for that goal. Use a separate private CV, cover letter, or tailored application narrative instead.

Do not mix the two channels.

## The rule

Public DraconDev should not say:

> “I want a job, but also I am building a business.”

Public DraconDev should say:

> “I build usable productized dev assets. The code and products prove the work. Aligned opportunities are welcome.”

That keeps the business signal clean while leaving room for employment conversations.

## What to stop saying publicly

Avoid language that chases the job bird:

- “Available for work.”
- “Open to opportunities.”
- “Hire me.”
- “Looking for a role.”
- “Job-seeking developer.”
- “Freelance developer.”
- “I can build your app.”
- “I teach developers.”
- “Learning journey.”
- “Course catalog.”
- “Full-stack developer looking for opportunities.”
- “I do everything: frontend, backend, AI, games, extensions, infra, consulting.”

Avoid language that makes the business bird look unserious:

- “Maybe I’ll monetize this someday.”
- “Just experimenting.”
- “Random side projects.”
- “Cool stuff I built.”
- “Founder” without product, users, pricing, or install pages.
- “AI platform for everyone” without a specific buyer and offer.

## What to keep saying publicly

Use language that is product-first and professional:

- “I make usable things people can run or play.”
- “Chrome extensions, games, tools, and Rust infrastructure.”
- “Use or buy the finished product when it is ready.”
- “Inspect public code if you want the proof.”
- “Build-with foundations are secondary.”
- “Private work is noted honestly, not promoted as public product.”
- “GitHub proves the maker; products carry the money path.”
- “Aligned product, platform, infra, or technical leadership opportunities are welcome.”

The last line is important. It does not beg for a job. It signals openness without changing the primary angle.

## How to frame the business angle honestly

Do not pretend the business is bigger than it is.

Use honest framing:

- “Productized assets in progress.”
- “Usable tools and extensions.”
- “Public code as proof.”
- “Private products not yet promoted.”
- “Verified install/play pages only.”
- “Revenue path through products, licenses, subscriptions, and support.”
- “Employment opportunities are secondary and must align with product ownership.”

Do not overclaim:

- Do not claim traction without numbers.
- Do not claim revenue without evidence.
- Do not claim public product status without install/play pages.
- Do not claim founder status unless the product/company structure is real.
- Do not claim enterprise readiness unless validation and support are real.

## Why this can make DraconDev unemployable on purpose

A conventional employer often wants a candidate who appears:

- available,
- manageable,
- replaceable,
- role-shaped,
- dependent on salary,
- willing to follow a company roadmap,
- unlikely to leave for their own product.

DraconDev’s stronger business signal says the opposite:

- independent,
- product-oriented,
- owner-minded,
- hard to manage in a narrow role,
- likely to keep building outside work,
- more interested in leverage than tasks,
- likely to leave if the product/business path works.

That is unattractive to the wrong employer.

It is attractive to the right one.

## The right employer

The right employer should read the profile and think:

> “This person ships real things. They understand product, code, users, and ownership. If we give them meaningful technical responsibility, they can create leverage.”

The wrong employer will think:

> “This person does not look like a normal employee.”

That is useful information.

## Profile implications

The GitHub profile should not ask for employment.

It should make the visitor think:

> “This person builds products and serious systems.”

Not:

> “This person wants a job.”

The profile can still help with employment indirectly. Strong product proof can attract better opportunities than generic job-hunt language.

But the public profile should not dilute the business angle to comfort conventional hiring filters.

## Docs implications

Docs should be split by audience:

### Public product/business docs

Use for GitHub, `dracon.uk`, and public-facing strategy:

- `README.md`
- `DIRECTION.md`
- `STRATEGIC_ANGLE_DECISION.md`
- `CHASING_TWO_BIRDS.md`
- `BUSINESS_POSITIONING_STRATEGY.md`
- `PRODUCT_INVENTORY.md`

These should preserve product/business positioning.

### Private job-search docs

Use only when applying for specific roles:

- tailored CV,
- cover letter,
- private hiring narrative,
- private project summaries translated into team impact,
- private references and work history.

These can reframe product work as employment evidence, but they should not contaminate the public business profile.

## Translation for job applications

When a specific job application requires employment framing, translate product work like this:

| Public product/business framing | Private job-application framing |
|:--------------------------------|:--------------------------------|
| “I build usable productized dev assets.” | “I design, build, validate, and ship developer tools end to end.” |
| “Products carry the money path.” | “I understand user problems, delivery, maintenance, and measurable outcomes.” |
| “GitHub proves the maker.” | “Public code demonstrates ownership, testing discipline, and engineering judgment.” |
| “Browser extensions and tools.” | “Cross-functional product engineering across browser UX, backend services, automation, and dev tooling.” |
| “Aligned opportunities welcome.” | “I am interested in roles where product ownership and technical depth matter.” |

Do not use the job-application framing on the public profile.

## What this means for DraconDev now

DraconDev should accept that business-first positioning narrows the audience.

That is the point.

The goal is not to be maximally employable. The goal is to become less dependent on employment while attracting better-aligned opportunities.

The public stance should be:

> “I am building productized dev assets. If an employer values that kind of ownership, we should talk. If they need a conventional employee signal, this public profile is not optimized for that.”

## Hard constraints

- Do not chase both birds equally.
- Do not make the public profile job-hunt language.
- Do not pretend business is already solved if revenue/traction is unverified.
- Do not hide the business angle to appear more employable.
- Do not use “founder” language without product evidence.
- Do not turn GitHub into a CV.
- Do not turn `dracon.uk` into a resume.
- Do not use private job-search framing in public docs.
